FAQ

Quick safari FAQ reference. Open a section, then tap a question to view the answer.

General Information
Do I need a visa to enter a new country?

Visa requirements vary by destination and passport. Prominent Africa recommends arranging visas before travel when possible. Some destinations may also allow visas on arrival at international airports or land borders. Keep passport photos and entry documents accessible.

What should I do if my flight gets delayed?

Contact Prominent Africa as soon as possible if your arrival changes. The local team can adjust transfer plans when they are informed in time.

Will a guide or representative meet me at the airport?

Yes. Please share flight details at least two to three weeks before arrival so the local representative can coordinate your greeting after customs.

What should I do if my luggage arrives late or gets lost?

Complete the airline’s lost luggage form before leaving the airport. Give the claim information to the Prominent Africa representative, who can help coordinate delivery when the bag arrives.

How long is it from the airport to the hotel?

Transfer time depends on the airport, hotel location, traffic, and arrival time. Your final itinerary will show the expected transfer flow when timings are confirmed.

Money Matters
Which currency is accepted?

Local currency is helpful for small purchases. U.S. dollars and Euros are often accepted at larger hotels, lodges, and tourist-facing shops. Your guide can advise when local currency is best.

Should I bring cash or credit cards?

Bring both. Credit cards are useful in major cities, airports, and larger lodges, but cash is still helpful for smaller purchases, gratuities, and places where card machines are unavailable.

Can I use ATMs?

ATMs are usually available in major cities and airports, but they should not be your only source of spending money. Remote safari areas may not have reliable ATM access.

How much cash should I bring?

This depends on personal spending habits, souvenirs, gratuities, and any optional purchases. Prominent Africa can provide trip-specific guidance before departure.

Safety & Health Matters
Is safari travel safe?

Safari travel is generally very well managed when you follow your guide’s instructions, stay aware of your surroundings and respect wildlife and park rules. Your guide and lodge teams are the best source for local safety guidance during the trip.

Can I get out of the safari vehicle?

Only when your guide says it is safe. Wildlife areas are not like zoos, and animals can be closer than they appear. Always wait for guide instructions before opening doors or stepping away from the vehicle.

Do I need vaccinations or malaria medication?

Health requirements vary by destination, season and personal medical history. Please consult a travel-medicine professional before departure and follow current destination-specific guidance.

Can I drink the water?

Use bottled or filtered water unless your guide or lodge specifically confirms that tap water is safe. Most safari lodges provide drinking water in rooms, vehicles, or dining areas.

What happens if I get sick or injured?

Tell your guide or lodge manager right away. They can help coordinate local medical care, adjust plans if needed, and contact Prominent Africa for support.

Should I buy travel insurance?

Yes. Travel insurance is strongly recommended for international safari travel, including coverage for medical needs, evacuation, delays, and unexpected trip disruption.

💵 Gratuities & Tipping Guide

Gratuities are an important part of the hospitality and tourism industry throughout East Africa and are a meaningful way to recognize exceptional service. While tipping is always at your discretion, it is customary and greatly appreciated by the guides, lodge staff, and hospitality teams who help make your journey memorable.

The recommendations below reflect customary practices throughout East Africa and are intended as a helpful guide rather than an expectation.

Rwanda Mountain Gorilla Trekking
Gorilla Trekking Guide

Our Recommendation: $10–25 USD per person

Your guide leads the trek, shares knowledge about the gorillas and their habitat, helps keep the group safe, and coordinates with the trackers throughout the experience.

Gorilla Trackers

Our Recommendation: $5 USD per person

Trackers enter the forest before your group to locate the assigned gorilla family and remain in contact with your guide. Their work is essential to both the trekking experience and long-term conservation.

Gorilla Trekking Porter (Highly Recommended)

Our Recommendation: $20 USD per person

A porter can carry your daypack or camera equipment and provide valuable support on steep, muddy, or uneven terrain. Hiring a porter makes the trek safer and more comfortable while providing meaningful employment that supports local communities and conservation.

Additional Guidance
  • Bring newer U.S. dollar bills issued in 2009 or later.
  • Carry a variety of small denominations, especially $1, $5, $10, and $20 bills.
  • Cash is generally preferred for gratuities throughout East Africa.
  • Local currency is also appropriate for restaurants, taxis, and smaller gratuities.
  • Check restaurant and hotel bills for an included service charge.
  • Lodge and camp staff generally share a communal tip box.
  • Safari guides are usually tipped directly at the conclusion of your safari.
  • A sincere "Asante" ("Thank you" in Swahili) is always appreciated.
  • If you're ever uncertain, simply ask us—we're always happy to help.

A Final Note

Above all, gratuities should reflect your personal experience. While these recommendations represent customary practices throughout East Africa, exceptional service may inspire additional recognition, and every gratuity is a meaningful expression of appreciation for the people who work hard to make your journey unforgettable.
